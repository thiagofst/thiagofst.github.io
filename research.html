Research Overview

One central focus of my research concerns exoplanets orbiting chemically and kinematically peculiar stellar populations—especially thick disc and halo stars—as these systems serve as unique laboratories for studying planet formation in the early Galaxy under low-metallicity and α-enhanced conditions. I am particularly interested in how the occurrence rates, bulk compositions, and orbital architectures of such planets may differ from those around younger stellar populations, as well as the implications of host star chemistry and Galactic environment on planetary systems' dynamical evolution (Ferreira & Galarza, 2025; arXiv:2512.17072). Recent works have also investigated warm Neptunes located in the so-called "Sub-Jovian desert", including the prospects for atmospheric characterisation of planets around old, low-mass stars and constraints on hydrodynamic escape processes (Ferreira & Galarza, in preparation).

Furthermore, a second line of research explores the orbital dynamics and demographics of transiting brown dwarf companions, whose specific focus is the eccentricity distribution of these substellar objects, and what is revealed about their origins—whether formed via disc fragmentation or extreme core accretion—and subsequent migration (Ferreira & Rice, in preparation). We examine how orbital eccentricity correlates with mass, host star properties, and separation, as well as the influence of tidal interactions with the host star in shaping long-term orbital evolution, which includes quantifying eccentricity damping and spin-orbit realignment timescales, and how these depend on stellar structure and age.

Finally, another active line of research—and a central focus of my PhD thesis—involves exploring the lives and post-main-sequence evolution of the earliest stars formed in the Universe, known as Population III stars, and their potential remnants. Using detailed hydrodynamic stellar evolution models computed with MESA, we establish a self-consistent evolutionary framework for low-mass, zero-metallicity stars, showing that non-canonical mixing and internal structural readjustments generically induce self-enrichment during advanced evolutionary phases (Ferreira et al., submitted to AAS Journals, November 2025; Paper I), and we further demonstrate that these processes leave unambiguous asteroseismic imprints encoded in the stars' internal stratification, enabling, for the first time, a seismic pathway to identifying and characterising primordial stellar populations (Ferreira et al., submitted to AAS Journals, December 2025; Paper II). In parallel, we explore whether the abundance patterns observed in extremely metal-poor stars reflect enrichment by core-collapse or pair-instability supernovae, and how such signatures might be detected in current or future surveys.
